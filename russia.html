A Calendar of Celebration

January

New Year Holidays (Jan 1-8)

[cite_start]

The biggest holiday celebration in Russia, including gift-giving, "New Year trees" (Novogodnyaya yolka), and visits from Ded Moroz (Grandfather Frost)[cite: 87, 88].

Orthodox Christmas (Jan 7)

[cite_start]

A deeply religious holiday following the Julian calendar, celebrated with family feasts and church services[cite: 89, 90].

Old New Year (Jan 13-14)

[cite_start]

An informal, traditional holiday marking the New Year according to the old Julian calendar, serving as a relaxed, nostalgic end to the holiday season[cite: 91, 92].

Epiphany (Jan 19)

[cite_start]

A major Orthodox Christian holiday famous for the tradition of "ice-hole bathing," where people dip into freezing, cross-shaped pools of water[cite: 93, 94].

February

Defender of the Fatherland Day (Feb 23)

[cite_start]

A public holiday that originally honored the military but has evolved into an unofficial "Men's Day," celebrating all men[cite: 95, 96].

Maslenitsa (Pancake Week) (Feb 24 - Mar 2)

[cite_start]

A folk festival marking the end of winter[cite: 97]. [cite_start]It involves eating blini (pancakes), folk celebrations, and burning an effigy of "Lady Maslenitsa" to welcome spring[cite: 98].

March

International Women's Day (Mar 8)

[cite_start]

A major public holiday to celebrate all women with gifts, flowers, and special attention, similar to Mother's Day and Valentine's Day combined[cite: 100, 101].

April

Orthodox Easter (Apr 20)

[cite_start]

The most important religious holiday in the Orthodox faith[cite: 103]. [cite_start]Celebrations include midnight church services, painting eggs, and baking special kulich (Easter bread)[cite: 104].

May

Spring and Labour Day (May 1)

[cite_start]

A public holiday marked with parades, concerts, and family outings to celebrate the arrival of spring[cite: 106, 107].

Victory Day (May 9)

[cite_start]

A very important national holiday commemorating the victory over Nazi Germany in WWII[cite: 108]. [cite_start]It features massive military parades, especially in Moscow, and fireworks[cite: 109].

White Nights Festival (Late May - July)

[cite_start]

A famous arts festival in St. Petersburg during the season of the midnight sun, including ballet, opera, and music events[cite: 110, 111].

June

Russia Day (Jun 12)

[cite_start]

The national holiday of the Russian Federation, celebrated with concerts, official ceremonies, and fireworks[cite: 113, 114].

November

Unity Day (Nov 4)

[cite_start]

A public holiday commemorating a 17th-century popular uprising that ended a period of political crisis[cite: 116, 117].

December

Russian Winter Festival (Mid-Dec - Mid-Jan)

[cite_start]

A broad cultural festival, especially in Moscow, including ice sculptures, Christmas markets, and winter activities at parks like Gorky Park[cite: 119, 120].
